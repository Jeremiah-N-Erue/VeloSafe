The Problem

Urban Mobility in Lagos is Broken

Lagos is the world's #1 most congested city in 2025 — yet the platforms serving its 17.8M+ residents are defined by safety failures, opacity, and missed economic opportunity.

🚨
#1

Lagos: the world's most dangerous commute

Lagos ranked #1 globally for traffic congestion in 2025 (TomTom Traffic Index) — with residents losing over ₦4 trillion annually to gridlock. Yet ride-hailing platforms remain poorly regulated, with Lagos forcing Uber into compliance over safety data-sharing failures in 2024.

⚡
3–5×

Surge pricing unpredictability

Incumbents levy aggressive surge multipliers during peak hours, eroding rider trust and pricing out lower-income commuters. Nigeria's FCCPC launched formal investigations into Bolt and Uber pricing practices in 2024.

🚗
25–30%

Crippling driver commissions

Bolt and Uber extract up to 30% from every fare. Drivers staged mass protests across Nigeria in 2024–25 over unsustainable earnings — leading to service degradation and rising driver churn across the Lagos market.

🏪
56%

SMEs locked out of mobility

56% of Nigerian MSMEs rely solely on social media to sell — with no access to embedded commerce within mobility platforms. Nigeria's $9.35B e-commerce market remains disconnected from the millions of daily ride-hailing interactions.

FAQ

Common Investor Questions

Straight answers to the questions we hear most often in investor conversations.

Why can't Bolt or Uber just copy VeloSafe's safety features?

They can add features, but they cannot change their incentive structure. Bolt and Uber's revenue models depend on surge pricing and high driver commissions — the very things that create distrust. VeloSafe is architectured from day one around safety and driver fairness. Incumbents changing those fundamentals would cannibalise their own revenue. Speed-to-market in a trust category also matters: the first platform to establish a verified safety record earns a durable brand moat.

How does VeloSafe make money at 20% commission?

Unit economics work because lower commission drives higher driver retention — reducing the costly churn and re-onboarding cycle that plagues competitors. Additionally, VeloSafe has planned ancillary revenue streams: in-app safety insurance (₦500–₦1,000 per trip opt-in), B2B corporate ride accounts with a 15% platform fee, and a future driver credit product backed by trip-history data. The 20% base commission is a competitive acquisition tool; the business model is multi-layered.

What's the plan if a larger player acquires a competitor to block VeloSafe?

Consolidation in this space actually benefits VeloSafe. When large players merge, driver and rider churn spikes as service quality drops during integration. Our strategy is to be the trusted alternative at exactly that moment. We are also deliberately building regulatory goodwill and community relationships that a foreign-headquartered acquirer cannot easily replicate. Local trust is the moat.

Why start with Lagos Island–Lekki–VI and not all of Lagos?

The Lagos Island–Lekki–Victoria Island corridor is the highest-density, highest-income commuter corridor in Nigeria. Riders there have the strongest willingness to pay for safety, the highest smartphone penetration, and the most consistent demand patterns. Winning this corridor first gives us verified trip data, word-of-mouth credibility, and the unit economics to prove the model before expanding to higher-complexity, lower-income corridors.

What happens if the $500K pre-seed doesn't close in full?

We have a staged deployment plan. At $300K we execute a focused MVP pilot — ride-hailing only, 50 verified drivers, 200 active riders on the VI–Lekki stretch — generating live safety data and traction metrics for a Seed pitch. At $500K we run the full platform launch including the commerce marketplace and sustain an 18-month runway. We have also applied for Google Cloud AI startup credits ($350K), which if granted, significantly reduces infrastructure costs and extends our runway regardless of close size.
